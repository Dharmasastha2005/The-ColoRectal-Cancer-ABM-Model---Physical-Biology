**A Clinically-Inspired Hybrid Agent-Based Model of Colorectal Cancer Growth and Regression Under Fractionated Radiotherapy with Immune and Metabolic Feedback**

While clinical trials remain the gold standard for evaluating oncologic therapies, they are often constrained by high costs, ethical limitations, recruitment challenges, and difficulties in controlling for the vast biological heterogeneity across patients. These limitations are particularly acute in radiation oncology, where personalising treatment protocols, such as dose per fraction, total dose, and combinations with systemic therapies, must balance efficacy with toxicity. Furthermore, traditional in vivo and in vitro models fail to fully recapitulate the spatial and temporal complexity of the tumor microenvironment (TME), especially when accounting for evolving oxygenation gradients, immune infiltration, and dynamic treatment responses.

Moreover, ABMs can be integrated with biophysical and pharmacokinetic models, such as the linear-quadratic (LQ) model for radiation-induced cell death, reaction-diffusion equations for oxygen transport, and ODE-based immune dynamics, to construct multiscale hybrid simulations. 

Such models allow researchers to test the effects of varying radiotherapy fractionation schedules, chemotherapy-induced radiosensitization, immune checkpoint inhibitors, or patient-specific clinical parameters (e.g., ECOG, comorbidities) in silico—well before clinical implementation.

In summary, this study leverages an agent-based, biologically grounded, clinically inspired modeling approach to recreate the progression and treatment of Stage III sigmoid colon adenocarcinoma. By incorporating radiobiological principles, immune-tumor interactions, ATP metabolism, hypoxia dynamics, and real-world clinical considerations, the model lays the groundwork for future virtual trials, parameter sensitivity testing, and AI-guided treatment planning.
